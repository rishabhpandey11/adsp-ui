Chapter 12 : Lab

Exercise 1: Wiener Filter Design for Audio Denoising

Goal: Design and implement a Wiener filter to denoise an audio signal corrupted by additive white Gaussian noise.

Task:

  • Load an audio signal and add white Gaussian noise with SNR = 10 dB
  • Design a Wiener filter with L=12 coefficients using the pseudo-inverse method
  • Apply the filter to denoise the signal
  • Compare the original, noisy, and denoised signals (plot and listen)
  • Compute and compare the mean squared error for noisy and denoised signals
  • Plot the impulse response and frequency response of the Wiener filter

Questions:

1. How does the filter length L affect the denoising performance?

2. What is the shape of the frequency response? Why does it make sense for speech?

3. How does the Wiener filter MSE compare to the noisy signal MSE?

Exercise 2: Matched Filter for Signal Detection

Goal: Implement a matched filter to detect a known signal embedded in noise.

Task:

  • Create a known signal (e.g., a chirp or a pulse train)
  • Embed the signal at a random position in white noise
  • Design the matched filter (time-reversed signal)
  • Apply the matched filter to detect the signal
  • Find the peak location and estimate the signal position
  • Plot the noisy signal and the matched filter output
  • Vary the noise level and observe the detection performance

Questions:

1.How does the matched filter output SNR compare to the input SNR?

2. What happens to detection accuracy as noise increases?

3.Why is the peak delayed relative to the actual signal position?

Exercise 3: Linear Prediction for Sinusoidal Signals

Goal: Design a linear predictor for a sinusoidal signal and verify the theoretical predictor coefficients.

Task:

  1. Generate a sinusoidal signal with a known frequency Ω.
  2. Compute the theoretical predictor coefficients for order p = 2:
    • a1 = 2 * cos(Ω)
    • a2 = -1
  3. Compute the predictor coefficients from the data using the least squares method.
  4. Compare the theoretical and estimated coefficients.
  5. Use the predictor to predict future samples.
  6. Compute the prediction error and plot it.
  7. Experiment with different frequencies and predictor orders.

Questions:

1.How close are the estimated coefficients to the theoretical values?

2. What happens if you use a higher predictor order (e.g., p=4)?

3. How does the phase of the signal affect the predictor coefficients?

Exercise 4: Linear Predictive Coding (LPC) for Speech

Goal: Implement an LPC encoder and decoder for speech compression.

Task:

  1. Load a speech signal.
  2. Divide the signal into frames (e.g., 30 ms frames).
  3. For each frame, compute LPC coefficients (order p = 12).
  4. Compute the residual (prediction error) for each frame.
  5. Reconstruct the speech from LPC coefficients and residual.
  6. Compare original and reconstructed speech (plot and listen).
  7. Quantize the residual and observe the effect on quality.

Questions:

1. How does the reconstructed speech sound compared to the original?

2. What is the compression ratio (considering you need to transmit LPC coefficients and residual)?

3. How does the predictor order p affect the quality?
