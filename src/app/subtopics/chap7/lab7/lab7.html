Chapter 7 : Lab

Objective

Apply z-transform analysis and filter design concepts through hands-on Python exercises. You will implement filters, analyze their properties, and visualize their behavior in time and frequency domains.

Exercise 1: Understanding the Shift Property

Goal: Verify that a delay in the time domain corresponds to multiplication by z to the power minus one in the z-domain.

Task: Implement both quantizer types and compare their behavior for signals centered around zero.

  • Create a simple signal: x = [1, 2, 3, 4]
  • Create a delayed version: x_delayed = [0, 1, 2, 3, 4]
  • Manually compute their z-transforms as polynomials
  • Verify the shift property

Questions:

1. Does X_delayed(z) = X(z) multiplied by z to the power minus one hold?

2. What happens if you delay the signal by 2 samples?

Exercise 2: FIR Filter Design - Moving Average

Goal: Implement and analyze a simple moving average FIR filter.

Task: Create a 5-point moving average filter and analyze its frequency response.

Questions:

1. What type of filter is this (low-pass, high-pass, or band-pass)?

2. Why does the moving average attenuate high frequencies?

3. What is the trade-off when increasing M?

Exercise 3: IIR Filter - First Order Low-Pass

Goal: Implement the simple IIR filter from section 7.2.4 and study stability.

Task: Implement y(n) = x(n) + p multiplied by y(n minus 1) for different values of p and observe stability.

Questions:

1. What happens when the absolute value of p is less than 1? What happens when it is greater than 1?

2. Can you relate the pole position to the impulse response behavior?

3. Why is the case p = 0.99 close to instability?

Exercise 4: Pole-Zero Analysis

Goal: Design filters by placing poles and zeros, then visualize with pole-zero plots.

Task: Create a notch filter to remove a specific frequency (for example, 50 Hz power line noise).

Questions:

1. What happens if poles are exactly on the unit circle?

2. How does the parameter r affect the notch width?

3. Design a notch filter for 60 Hz power line noise (assume sampling frequency equals 1000 Hz).
